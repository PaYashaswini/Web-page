Syllogism is a form of logical reasoning that uses deductive reasoning to arrive at a conclusion based on two or more propositions (called premises) that are assumed to be true. Syllogisms are widely used in reasoning tests, especially in competitive exams, to test logical thinking skills.

Types of Syllogisms

A.Categorical Syllogism:
Deals with statements about categories or classes.
Example:
1. All cats are animals.
2. Some animals are pets.
3. Therefore, some pets are cats. (The conclusion is logically correct or incorrect depending on the premises’ structure.)

B. Hypothetical Syllogism:
Uses "if... then" statements.
Example: 1. If it rains, the ground will be wet. 2. It is raining. 3. Therefore, the ground is wet.

C. Disjunctive Syllogism: Based on an "either... or" statement.
Example: 1. John will go to the party or he will stay home. 2.John is not going to the party. 3.Therefore, John will stay home.

Common Terms in Syllogism

1. All: A universal affirmative statement (e.g., "All A are B"). 2. Some: A particular affirmative statement (e.g., "Some A are B"). 3. No: A universal negative statement (e.g., "No A are B"). 4.Some Not: A particular negative statement (e.g., "Some A are not B").

Rules to Follow for Valid Syllogism Conclusions

a.Two Negatives Cannot Yield a Positive Conclusion: If both premises are negative, no valid conclusion can be drawn. b.Middle Term Must Be Distributed at Least Once: The middle term (the term common to both premises) must be universal in at least one of the premises. c.Distribution of Terms: A term distributed in the conclusion must be distributed in the premise.

Tipcs and Tricks

1. Understand the Structure: Familiarize yourself with how terms are distributed in premises. 2. Practice Drawing Venn Diagrams: Visual representation can help understand relationships. 3. Look for Keywords: Words like “all,” “some,” “no,” and “not” give clues about the conclusion. 4. Check Rules: Ensure the conclusion doesn’t contradict any rules for a valid syllogism.
